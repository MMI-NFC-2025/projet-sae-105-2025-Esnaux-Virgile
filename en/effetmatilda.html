What is the Matilda Effect?

The Matilda Effect in brief

The Matilda Effect refers to the fact that women scientists often see their work minimized or attributed to men. This phenomenon, named by historian Margaret Rossiter in 1993, reminds us that women's contributions are often made invisible. For example, despite their major discoveries, women are rarely recognized, which perpetuates an imbalance in science. Recognizing this effect is important to fairly value women's merit and promote a more just and inclusive science.

« Imagination is the faculty of discovery, par excellence. It is the one that penetrates the invisible worlds that surround us, the worlds of science. »

Quote from Ada Lovelace

Why does it exist?

The main causes of the Matilda Effect are gender stereotypes, the low presence of women in science, discrimination in institutions, as well as conscious or unconscious biases favoring men. This inequality is also seen in scientific citations and the low attribution of prizes like the Nobel.
